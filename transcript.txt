This is my way of avoiding the terrorism trap. There are acts of political violence which, for a variety of reasons, fall short of our legal definition of terrorism that nonetheless may meet the threshold for being a national security concern. The use of the term violent extremism captures this, where terrorism may not. Canada is no stranger to violent extremism. Throughout its history, a diverse number of violent movements have operated within Canada or used our country as a base from which to export terrorism abroad. This includes the Irish Fenians, the Sons of Freedom, a splinter group of the Doukhobors a Russian religious sect that engaged in a campaign of arson and bombings in the 1920s through to the 1960s. And in the 1960s the Front de Liberation du Quebec, the FLQ, a violent extremist separatist group that was responsible for hundreds of acts of violent extremism, including a series of bombings against a range of targets, which killed at least six individuals. The most famous incident occurred in October 1970, when the group organized the kidnapping of British Trade Commissioner James Cross and Quebec Labor Minister Pierre Laporte. The latter was killed a few days into the crisis. As a result of these actions, public support for the FLQ plummeted. Although the movement continued to engage in bombing throughout Quebec, the movement was largely infiltrated by 1973 and dispersed. By the 1980s, Canada experienced what may be called transnational or spillover terrorism. Essentially, transnational terrorism is the internationalization of an essentially domestic dispute. Between the 1960s and the 1990s, Canada's experience of spillover violent extremism was mostly in the realm of facilitation and financing groups abroad. Entities, such as the Irish Republican Army, the IRA, for example, used Canada as a platform for fundraising. Nevertheless, there were serious acts of violence. For example, between the 1960s through to the 1980s, anti-Castro Cubans in the United States and Canada engaged in a series of 12 attacks, resulting in one death and several persons injured, as well as serious property damage. There were also acts of violence associated with Armenian and Tamil extremist groups, as well as Sikh extremist groups, including Air India Flight 182, which killed 331 persons on board, including 268 Canadian citizens. The 1990s saw the rise of what would become known as Al Qaeda and eventually Islamic State-inspired extremism. The case of Ahmed Rassam, the Millennium Bomber, was indicative of this. Rassam was caught entering the United States from Canada, with explosives in the trunk of his car for the purpose of attacking the Los Angeles airport in December 1999. In the post-911 era, Al Qaeda and Islamic State-inspired extremism became what is now described as a homegrown threat. The most famous of these cases is the so-called Toronto 18 case, which saw a number of individuals charged for planning attacks against the Toronto Stock Exchange and various security and defense-related targets. With the start of the Syrian Civil War, the threat from Al Qaeda and Islamic State extremists changed once again. Now it was Canadians who were seeking travel overseas to join violent extremist groups abroad. This raised concerns that individuals who traveled overseas could be radicalized further and trained in battle, making future plots against Canadians more deadly and effective. However, at the time of recording this video, it appears that the fear that dozens, if not hundreds of foreign fighters might seek to return to Canada has not materialized. Another form of extremism comes from state-backed groups such as Hisbala, which is backed by Iran, and Lashkar-e-Tayyiba, the L-E-T, a Pakistan-based violent extremist group. Within Canada, these groups tend to fundraise and recruit Canadians to engage in supporting violent extremism abroad. Finally, more recently, there has been concern over far-right extremism and single-issue extremism in Canada. A challenge in fighting these groups is that they really aren't motivated by a single ideology so much as they are a collection of grievances. For the purposes of this segment, I will use the definition provided by Professors Barbara Perry and Ryan Scrivins, who describe far-right extremism as "...a loose movement, animated by a racially, ethnically, and sexually defined nationalism. This nationalism is typically framed in terms of white power and is grounded in xenophobic and exclusionary understandings of the perceived threats posed by groups such as non-whites, Jews, immigrants, homosexuals, and feminists." In support of these beliefs, far-right extremist groups are willing to assume both an offensive and defensive stance in the interest of preserving their so-called heritage and their so-called homeland. Importantly, although it has received a lot of recent attention, far-right extremist violence in Canada is not new. In the 1960s, the Toronto-based Edmund Burke Society, an anti-left and anti-communist group that called for militant conservative activism, attacked Vietnam war protesters and gatherings of labor groups and Marxists. This group later became the more overtly racist and anti-Semitic Western Guard, but its membership declined in the 1970s. Canada also has branches of the Ku Klux Klan, or the KKK, a racist, nativist, and anti-Semitic group that came to Canada via the United States in the 1920s. There was a resurgence in the group's activities in the 1970s and 1980s, but this was forestalled by the conviction and imprisonment of much of its leadership. However, there are signs that the Klan and other people sympathetic to its aims exist in Canada today. In 2017, flyers claiming to be from the organization were distributed in British Columbia. Other far-right groups with historical presence in Canada include Skinheads, the so-called Creativity Movement, a Christian extremist group which seeks to create an ethnically-based religion to advance the white race that developed the concept of racial holy war, or what's sometimes called Rohoa. Today far-right extremism, or at least the broad array of groups that we can collect under this label, come from a variety of sources. I think it is fair to argue that these groups constitute a far-right ecosystem that overlaps and feeds off of each interaction. This includes cultural chauvinists and misogynist groups such as the Proud Boys, who are known to contribute to violence at political rallies and target indigenous groups through violent neo-Nazi gangs.
